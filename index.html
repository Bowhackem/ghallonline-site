Origins

I learn how things work. Then I build things. That's been true since long before it was a career, taking apart what's in front of me, whether it's a Linux box, a fungal network, or a spreadsheet, until I understand the system underneath it.

I work across technology, infrastructure, automation, and design, and just as seriously across botany, mycology, and business systems, not as separate hobbies, but as the same instinct pointed in different directions.

This site is part of that practice too. It's not finished, and it's not meant to be. I'd rather keep shaping something alive than finish something static.

How I Think

Understand before you trust

I don't take a system's word for how it works. I open it up first.

Systems, not silos

A server, a garden bed, and a P&L are all systems under pressure. The same questions apply to all three.

Build the smallest true version first

A rough thing that actually works teaches you more than a polished plan that doesn't exist yet.

Automate the boring, not the important

Automation should remove friction, not judgment.

Stay a beginner somewhere

I keep at least one discipline where I'm still clearly learning. It keeps the rest honest.

UCTools

Tools built from doing the same Cisco UCS work too many times.

The friction

Cisco UCS environments have a way of making you repeat yourself: the same profile checks, the same firmware verifications, the same manual steps that every engineer on the team eventually just knows, but nobody's written down. I kept hitting the same friction on the same kinds of tasks, and kept watching other engineers hit it too.

What I noticed

Most of what made the work slow wasn't the actual complexity of UCS. It was how much of the process lived in people's heads instead of in a tool. Tribal knowledge is fine until you need it fast, or until the person who has it is out.

What I built

UCTools started as a way to turn that tribal knowledge into something reusable: small, focused utilities that handle the repetitive parts of UCS work so the people using them can spend their attention on the part that actually needs judgment.

[Add measurable impact if available: time saved, adoption within the team, specific workflows improved]. At minimum, it meant the same problem didn't have to get solved from scratch by whoever ran into it next.

What it reveals

This is probably the clearest example of how I default to working: notice the friction, understand why it's actually happening, then build something that removes it for more than just me. It's the same instinct that shows up everywhere else on this site. I just recognize it most clearly here.

Sweet Beans Systems

Designing the systems behind a real service business, not just running one.

Sweet Beans is a grooming business, but treating it as just "a grooming business" misses the actual work. Underneath it is the same kind of systems problem I find everywhere else: scheduling that has to hold up against real constraints, customer communication that has to be clear and consistent, documentation that has to survive someone else needing to run things without me in the room.

I've been building the operational layer around it: workflow design, booking and scheduling, customer experience, branding, day-to-day documentation, and automation where it actually removes friction instead of adding a new thing to maintain. None of it is theoretical; it has to work on a Tuesday afternoon with a real client and a real dog in the room.

This is where the software side of what I do meets the physical, service-work side. A system that only works on a whiteboard isn't a system yet. Sweet Beans is where I find out whether mine actually survive contact with real customers and real constraints.

Early 2020

Coronavirus Diary

A small tool for tracking an uncertain moment.

In early 2020, information about COVID-19 was changing constantly, and it was hard to keep a clear picture of what was actually happening versus what was rumor or noise. I built Coronavirus Diary as a way to track and organize that information for myself: a small structure laid over a genuinely chaotic period.

It wasn't a medical tool and didn't try to be one: no diagnoses, no health guidance, no claims about outcomes. It was closer to a personal instinct: when the world gets hard to read, build something that makes it a little more legible, even in a small way.

Looking back, it's less about the tool itself and more about what it shows: that instinct to create structure under uncertainty shows up early, and it shows up often.
